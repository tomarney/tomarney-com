I am a palaeoceanographer -- that is, I study ancient oceans to investigate what the environment looked like thousands or millions of years ago.

The oceans are a critical part of the Earth system and so tend to either cause or react to most changes in Earth's climate. By reading the clues left in the rocks and fossils that have gradually carpeted the seafloor, layer by layer, we can tell when Antarctica got its ice; [work out](https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/science-environment-51150001) what happened when the dinosaur-killing Chixulub asteroid hit; and find when (and why) Earth was hotter than it is now, and what that might mean for our future.

This last point is why palaeoclimate science is so important at the moment -- looking at past change is a [very important](https://science.sciencemag.org/content/370/6517/eaay3701) way to make sure our models for future climate change are accurate.

At the moment I am studying for a PhD at the [National Oceanography Centre](https://www.noc.ac.uk), [University of Southampton](https://www.southampton.ac.uk/oes), and the [British Antarctic Survey](https://www.bas.ac.uk).
