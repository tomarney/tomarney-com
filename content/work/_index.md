I am a palaeoceanographer -- that is, I study ancient oceans to investigate what the environment looked like thousands or millions of years ago.

The oceans are a critical part of the Earth system and so tend to either cause or react to most changes in Earth's climate. By reading the clues left in the rocks and fossils that have gradually carpeted the seafloor, layer by layer, we can tell when Antarctica got its ice; [work out](https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/science-environment-51150001) what happened when the dinosaur-killing Chixulub asteroid hit; and find when (and why) Earth was hotter than it is now, and what that might mean for our future.

This last point is why palaeoclimate science is so important at the moment -- looking at past change is a [very important](https://science.sciencemag.org/content/370/6517/eaay3701) way to make sure our models for future climate change are accurate.

## Current research

At the moment I am studying for a PhD at the [National Oceanography Centre](https://www.noc.ac.uk), [University of Southampton](https://www.southampton.ac.uk/oes), and the [British Antarctic Survey](https://www.bas.ac.uk). My project concerns the early Pleistocene palaeoceanography of the West Antarctic margin, and at the moment I’m analysing a carbonate-rich sedimentary record from the Byrd Seamounts in the Amundsen Sea.

## Past work

My master's degree (also at Southampton), was originally going to be an analysis of {{<d11b>}} profiles from planktic foraminifera from a couple of IODP cruises, in order to investigate the extent of the effect of temperature on the depth of remineralisation. Unfortunately, the COVID-19 pandemic got in the way of that and I changed to using {{<d13c>}} gradients from previously published data, which involved the construction of a database of palaeoclimate data. The thesis is available [on github](https://github.com/t-arney/msc-thesis).

My undergraduate degree was a BSc in geology from Durham, which involved a six-week independent mapping project as a dissertation; I did mine on the spectacular folding and faulting of the carboniferous sediments in the coastal exposures in Berwick and Scremerston (Northumberland, north-east UK).
